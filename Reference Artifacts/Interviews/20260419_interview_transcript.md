## Key Takeaways

- **Property-level requirements are a gap in the MVP.** Person A flagged fire extinguishers, kitchen specs, signage, parking (e.g. 3 non-street spaces), and inspection rules as things he'd want surfaced alongside market-level legality. These determine whether a specific house qualifies even when the market allows STRs.
- **Home sharing vs. full STR is a distinct user intent.** Rules (and business-license requirements) diverge meaningfully between renting the whole place and renting part of an owner-occupied home. A toggle or filter on intent would narrow results to what's actually relevant.
- **Alerts should fire *before* legislation passes, not just after.** Person A wants to know when changes are being *discussed* at city council so he can show up and advocate — not just react to enforcement. This reframes the monitoring feature from defensive (avoid fines) to proactive (shape the rules).
- **Neighbor complaints, not city notifications, were the trigger event both times.** Both Virginia Beach and Danville compliance discoveries started with neighbor letters/complaints, not proactive city outreach. Suggests the "fresh data" pitch lands harder than a "we'll notify you of changes" pitch alone.
- **Biggest trust objection: "Why not just go to the county site?"** The product has to deliver something the official page can't — speed across multiple markets, pre-change monitoring, cross-jurisdiction comparison, or property-level applicability. A summary of one city's page isn't enough of a moat.
- **Source accuracy and link freshness are the core credibility challenge.** Person A surfaced this unprompted. The link-validation worker pipeline addresses it directly — worth making visible to users (e.g. "last verified 4 days ago" + "source link checked").
